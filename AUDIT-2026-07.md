## 0. The One Fact That Reframes Everything

This audit was commissioned using a brief that assumes 1Zero9 **is or should become** a "premium AI-powered Digital Product Studio." The live site, as of today, is the opposite: a solo personal portfolio ("the digital workshop of Stephen Cranfield") rebuilt from scratch three days ago (2026-07-04), in first-person voice throughout, with no services page, no pricing, no team, and no contact form. `/services` and `/contact` permanently redirect to the homepage and `/about`.

That contradiction alone would be a clean, single-axis finding — *"the brief doesn't match the site, pick one"* — except for one thing found during discovery: an **untracked file, `quote-calculator.html`, sitting at the repo root**, fully built, unlinked from any navigation, under a *third* brand name ("Steve — AI-Built Web Development"), in a *third* visual identity, pricing a *third* business model entirely: a small/local Irish web-design shop (€950–€15,000 tiers, WordPress/Wix migrations, .ie domain resale, Google Workspace mailbox resale).

So there are not two positions in tension — there are **three**, and two of them are already built to completion:

| | Personal Workshop (live) | "Digital Product Studio" (this brief's assumption) | Irish SMB Web Shop (orphaned calculator) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brand name | 1Zero9 | — (undefined) | "Steve — AI-Built Web Development" |
| Visual system | Black / hot-ember / Fraunces / Inter | — (undefined) | Paper-cream / teal / gold / IBM Plex |
| Buyer | Employers, collaborators | Enterprise & mid-market clients | Irish small businesses |
| Proof mechanism | Shipped projects, told honestly | Case studies, client logos, six-figure capacity | Tiered pricing, add-ons, care plans |
| Status | Live, shipped 2026-07-04 | Aspirational (this brief) | Fully built, never wired up, never decided |

Every section below scores the live site against this brief's studio-shaped questions as instructed. But the single highest-leverage finding of this entire audit is **Section 8** — the business has not decided which of these three companies it is, and one of the undecided options is already sitting in the git working tree with no owner, no access control, and no link to anything.

## 2. Repository / Engineering Audit (Phase 2)

**Architecture:** genuinely disciplined for its size. `src/lib/content.ts` is an explicit single choke-point for all content-collections access ("if the loader ever needs replacing, this file is the swap point"). `site.ts` centralizes nav/author/metadata as one typed object. `metadata.ts` gives every page canonical/OG/Twitter metadata from one helper. Only 2 client components exist in the entire app (`theme-provider`, `theme-toggle`) — everything else is server-rendered. This is a deliberately minimal-JS, maintainable codebase, not an accidentally simple one.

**Quality gates:** this is the weak point. `strict` TypeScript and Zod-validated MDX are real, enforced-at-build-time safeguards. But the colophon's public claim — *"Accessibility is verified with axe and a keyboard pass on every change; performance is gated at Lighthouse 95+"* — is **not backed by any automation in the repo**. With zero CI, that claim is a manual, unenforced, and currently unverifiable promise published as fact. For a site whose entire premise is "demonstrate capability, don't describe it," this is the one place description has outrun demonstration.

**Security posture in the pipeline:** one high-severity, RCE-capable transitive dependency (`serialize-javascript` via `content-collections`) has a known fix that hasn't been applied. Combined with no CI and auto-deploy-on-push, a bad dependency bump or a bad commit reaches production with zero automated check in between.

**Risk assessment:** Low blast radius today (static site, no auth, no database, no forms — see Section 4's Security Consultant review for the full reasoning), but the *process* gaps (no CI, unpatched known CVE, unverified public quality claims) are exactly the things that break first the moment this codebase is handed to a second contributor or pushed past its current ~17-page scale.

**Priority technical fixes:** upgrade `@content-collections/core`; stand up a minimal CI job (lint + typecheck + build, half a day of work); add a `headers()` block for CSP/X-Frame-Options; remove the unused `motion` dependency; resolve the two untracked root-level files (commit with intent, or delete).

Benchmarked against two real, premium, top-tier **personal-brand** sites (chosen because the live 1Zero9 site is itself a personal-brand site, not because they run agencies) — Rauno Freiberg (interaction designer, Vercel/Devouring Details) and swyx.io (Shawn Wang, writer/founder/AI engineer):

This is the single most concrete, low-effort, high-leverage gap the benchmark surfaces, and it's fixable without resolving the studio-vs-workshop question at all.

## 5. SWOT

**Strengths:** disciplined, minimal-JS architecture; genuine design craft and brand coherence; two real live external products; strong technical SEO scaffolding; accessibility fundamentals that are unusually good for the site's size; honest, first-person case-study voice.

**Weaknesses:** no conversion mechanism of any kind; zero analytics; unresolved three-way business-model conflict, one leg of which (the calculator) is a live, unlinked, unowned artifact; unenforced public quality claims (colophon vs. actual CI); one unpatched high-severity dependency vulnerability; missing security headers; thin content volume (1 post, published today).

**Opportunities:** the benchmark shows a credible path exists (stated current focus + third-party validation) that requires no positioning decision at all; the calculator's pricing logic is real, reusable work if a commercial direction is ever chosen; the "site as its own case study" colophon device is a genuinely distinctive proof-of-craft mechanic few personal sites use.

**Threats:** the orphaned calculator surfacing (search index, shared link, screenshot) would contradict the entire brand instantly, with real-money pricing attached to a name ("Steve") that doesn't match the live brand; the unpatched RCE-class dependency; continuing to build content/features on top of an undecided business model compounds the eventual cost of resolving it.

## 6. Business Repositioning (Phase 5) — The Fork That Actually Matters

This audit was asked to evaluate the site against a studio-repositioning brief, contradictions included, and the scores above do that honestly: **as a studio, this site scores ~3–4/10 across every commercial dimension.** But the more useful output of this audit is naming the actual decision sitting unresolved in the repository, because no amount of copywriting fixes a site that's arguing with itself:

1. **Personal Workshop** (current, live, shipped 3 days ago, owner-approved as of the last rebuild) — optimizes for credibility with employers/collaborators through demonstrated craft. Benchmarked well against real top-tier precedent (Section 4). Needs: a current-focus line, third-party validation, nothing structural.
2. **Digital Product Studio** (this brief's assumption) — optimizes for enterprise/mid-market client acquisition. Needs, at minimum: named or quantified case-study outcomes, a real intake mechanism, security headers + CI (non-negotiable for any six-figure IT due-diligence pass), a stated process/capacity story. This is a materially larger, ongoing commitment, not a copy change.
3. **Irish SMB Web Shop** (the orphaned calculator) — optimizes for local small-business lead generation at fixed price tiers. Already has real pricing logic built. Would need its own IA, its own brand (or a very deliberate sub-brand decision), and directly conflicts with both other positions on tone, price-transparency, and target buyer.

**Recommendation, stated plainly:** these three cannot coexist much longer without cost. Options 1 and 2 can theoretically merge (a workshop that also states its capacity for larger engagements), but Option 3 is a genuinely different business — different buyer, different price point, different brand — bolting it onto either of the others would confuse both. The lowest-risk, highest-leverage move available today is to **explicitly decide, in writing, what happens to the calculator** (ship it as its own thing under its own brand, fold its logic into a real services page, or delete it) before doing anything else in this report. Every other recommendation below assumes that decision gets made.

## 7. Information Architecture, Content & Design Direction (Phases 6–8, condensed)

Regardless of which fork is chosen, these are true today:

- **IA:** current 4-route structure is appropriately shallow for a workshop; if any commercial direction is added, it should be one clearly-labeled addition (e.g., `/work-with-me` or similar), not a full service-hierarchy rebuild — over-building IA before the positioning decision is made would be premature.
- **Content:** the case-study format (problem → build → lesson) is strong and differentiated; the fastest content upgrade available is adding one quantified or third-party-validated data point per project, not rewriting the voice.
- **Design system:** no changes needed — the Creative & First Impression review scored this the highest of any dimension (8/10 UI). The two concrete fixes are removing/relocating the "in progress" status line and adding a portrait to About.
- **Component consistency:** unify the homepage card grid vs. `/projects` row layout for the same content type (flagged independently by the UX review).
